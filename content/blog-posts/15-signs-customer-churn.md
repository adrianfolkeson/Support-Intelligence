## The Silent Warning Signs

Every day, customers are quietly deciding to leave your SaaS product. They're not posting about it on Twitter. They're not telling their customer success manager. They're just... fading away.

By the time they ask to cancel, the decision has been made. The opportunity to save them has passed.

But what if you could see it coming? What if you could intervene while there's still time?

After analyzing millions of support tickets and customer interactions, we've identified the 15 clearest signs that a customer is about to churn—and what you can do about each one.

---

## The 15 Warning Signs of Customer Churn

### 1. Sudden Increase in Support Tickets

**The Sign:** A customer who rarely contacts support suddenly submits multiple tickets in a short period.

**What It Means:** They're experiencing frustration or problems, and their patience is wearing thin.

**What to Do:**
- Flag for immediate follow-up
- Escalate to senior support
- Check for systemic issues affecting their account

### 2. Declining Feature Usage

**The Sign:** Core feature usage drops by 30% or more over 30 days.

**What It Means:** They're losing interest or not finding value.

**What to Do:**
- Reach out with personalized tips
- Offer training on underused features
- Check if their needs have changed

### 3. Negative Sentiment in Communication

**The Sign:** Support tickets show increasingly frustrated language, negative sentiment, or angry tone.

**What It Means:** They're approaching their breaking point.

**What to Do:**
- Assign your best CS rep
- Address underlying issues, not just symptoms
- Offer compensation or goodwill gesture

### 4. Login Frequency Drops

**The Sign:** They're logging in half as often as usual.

**What It Means:** You're becoming forgettable, or they're actively looking for alternatives.

**What to Do:**
- Re-engagement campaign with value reminders
- Check if competitors have reached out
- Offer personalized check-in

### 5. Payment Failures

**The Sign:** Credit card declines or failed payment attempts.

**What It Means:** Financial stress or testing cancellation waters.

**What to Do:**
- Multiple payment retry attempts with grace periods
- Reach out personally to resolve
- Offer temporary discount if budget is issue

### 6. Competitor Mentions

**The Sign:** Asking about competitor features or comparing pricing.

**What It Means:** They're actively shopping alternatives.

**What to Do:**
- Address specific concerns directly
- Highlight your unique value props
- Offer win-back incentives

### 7. Seat Count Reduction

**The Sign:** Reducing number of user seats or licenses.

**What It Means:** Budget cuts or reduced team usage.

**What to Do:**
- Understand why (budget vs. underuse)
- Offer flexible pricing if appropriate
- Help them maximize remaining seats

### 8. No Engagement with Outreach

**The Sign:** Not responding to emails, calls, or in-app messages.

**What It Means:** They've checked out mentally.

**What to Do:**
- Try different channels (phone vs email)
- Send break-up email ("We haven't heard from you...")
- Accept that some churn is inevitable

### 9. Feature Requests Go Unanswered

**The Sign:** They stop requesting features or providing feedback.

**What It Means:** They've given up on your product roadmap.

**What to Do:**
- Proactively update them on relevant roadmap items
- Explain the "why" behind prioritization
- Show them you're listening

### 10. Executive Sponsor Leaves

**The Sign:** The champion who bought your product leaves the company.

**What It Means:** Your biggest internal advocate is gone.

**What to Do:**
- Immediately build relationship with replacement
- Demonstrate ongoing value to new stakeholder
- Offer to present success metrics to leadership

### 11. Contract Renewal Approaches (Silence)

**The Sign:** Renewal is 60 days out and they haven't responded to outreach.

**What It Means:** They're planning to leave but avoiding the conversation.

**What to Do:**
- Escalate to executive sponsor
- Offer renewal incentives early
- Understand their decision timeline

### 12. Usage Becomes Transactional

**The Sign:** They only log in to do the bare minimum, no exploration of new features.

**What It Means:** They're doing the bare minimum until they can switch.

**What to Do:**
- Reinforce value propositions
- Share case studies of similar companies
- Offer personalized success review

### 13. Negative NPS or CSAT Scores

**The Sign:** They give low scores on surveys but don't explain why.

**What It Means:** Passive dissatisfaction that's building up.

**What to Do:**
- Follow up immediately for details
- Don't be defensive—listen and learn
- Close the loop on improvements

### 14. Public Criticism

**The Sign:** Negative reviews, tweets, or forum posts about your product.

**What It Means:** They're angry and want others to know.

**What to Do:**
- Respond publicly and promptly
- Take conversation private to resolve
- Turn critic into advocate with exceptional service

### 15. Migration Questions

**The Sign:** Asking about data export or API access for integration.

**What It Means:** They're technically preparing to leave.

**What to Do:**
- This is the final stage—intervention is difficult
- Offer to address underlying concerns
- Make the process easy (leave on good terms for potential return)

## The Intervention Framework

When you identify an at-risk customer, follow this framework:

### **ASSESS** (Day 0)
- Gather all relevant data
- Understand the full picture
- Determine risk severity

### **REACH OUT** (Day 0-1)
- Personalized message from human
- Acknowledge their experience
- Offer to help

### **INVESTIGATE** (Day 1-3)
- Understand root cause
- Check for systemic issues
- Identify quick wins

### **ACT** (Day 3-7)
- Fix immediate problems
- Provide ongoing support
- Set success metrics

### **FOLLOW UP** (Ongoing)
- Monitor for improvement
- Adjust approach as needed
- Document learnings

## The Psychology of Churn Prevention

Understanding why customers leave helps you prevent it.

### **The 5 Stages of Churn:**

1. **Dissatisfaction** (Early warning signs appear)
   - *This is when intervention is most effective*

2. **Disengagement** (Checking out mentally)
   - *Still salvageable with effort*

3. **Exploration** (Looking at alternatives)
   - *Actively comparing options*

4. **Decision** (Made up mind to leave)
   - *Very difficult to reverse*

5. **Departure** (Cancelled)
   - *Focus on learnings for future*

**Key Insight:** Most companies intervene at stage 3 or 4. That's too late. You need to catch them at stage 1.

## Building Your Churn Prevention Playbook

Document your approach so the whole team can execute:

### **For Each Warning Sign:**
- Trigger criteria (what alerts you)
- Severity level (urgent vs monitoring)
- Owner role (who responds)
- Response playbook (steps to take)
- Success metrics (how to measure)

### **Example: Negative Sentiment Playbook**

**Trigger:** 3+ tickets with negative sentiment in 7 days

**Severity:** High

**Owner:** Senior Customer Success Manager

**Response:**
1. Review ticket history for patterns
2. Identify root cause
3. Reach out within 4 hours
4. Escalate bug reports immediately
5. Document in CRM

**Success:** Sentiment improves, issue resolved

## Common Mistakes in Churn Prevention

### ❌ **Waiting for Customers to Ask to Cancel**
By then, it's too late. Intervene at the first warning sign.

### ❌ **Treating All Customers the Same**
Segment by revenue, strategic value, and churn risk.

### ❌ **Focusing Only on Logo Churn**
Revenue churn matters more. Save the big accounts.

### ❌ **One-Size-Fits-All Interventions**
Personalize based on why they're at risk.

### ❌ **Ignoring Product Issues**
Customer success can't fix product-market fit problems.
